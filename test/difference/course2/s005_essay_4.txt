The lesson from my time management experience is straightforward. I was not managing my time. I was managing my feelings about my time, which is a different activity and a less useful one. Once I stopped treating busyness as a signal of productivity and started measuring actual output instead, the problem became much easier to address. The shift required no special insight or difficult emotion. It required a decision to track what I was doing and compare it against what I had intended. That comparison was enough. Reflection is only valuable when it changes behaviour. In this case, it did.
