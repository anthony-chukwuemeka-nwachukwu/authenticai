Three factors determine whether online learning produces positive outcomes for students. First, access to reliable infrastructure is a prerequisite for consistent engagement. Second, students must possess sufficient self-regulation to manage asynchronous workflows without external supervision. Third, assessment design must be aligned with the modality in order to avoid inflating apparent performance. When these three conditions are met, online learning can be evaluated on its merits. When one or more are absent, observed outcomes reflect confounding variables rather than the learning modality itself. Any policy recommendation that does not account for these conditions is likely to produce inconsistent results across different institutional and demographic contexts.
